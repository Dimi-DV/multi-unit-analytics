# Dataset design and provenance

Everything transactional in this repo is synthetic and generated by the seeded
Python package in `generator/`. The brand, locations, dishes, and vendors are
fictional (collision-checked against real businesses at authoring time). The
scale and the shape of the mess mirror the class of problems I worked on
professionally in multi-unit restaurant analytics; no real employer data,
names, or figures appear anywhere in this repository or its git history.

## Why synthetic, and why it is disclosed

No public dataset carries multi-location restaurant financials (comp sales,
prime cost, menu mix, POS-to-GL reconciliation). Rebuilding the domain
synthetically means every join key, every anomaly, and every business rule in
this repo has an author who can defend it. The generation design below is that
disclosure: the mess is engineered, parameterized, and documented, which is
what makes it a demonstration of cleaning skill rather than noise.

## Shape

Fictional group: Copperwick, a 9-location NYC all-day American bistro group,
2024-01-01 through 2026-06-30 (a constant; the generator never reads the
clock). Four raw fact feeds and eight reference/dimension seeds, loaded into an
all-TEXT `raw` schema (schema-on-read: typing is the first transformation, done
in the staging layer).

| Feed | Grain | ~Rows |
|---|---|---|
| `raw.pos_ticket_lines` | one exported POS line, dirty on purpose | 4.85M |
| `raw.ap_invoice_lines` | one vendor invoice line (COGS as purchases) | 68K |
| `raw.labor_daily` | location x business date x role | 50K |
| `raw.gl_daily_sales` | location x business date (finance book) | 7.5K |

Committed seeds (`seeds/`): locations (10 rows for 9 stores, see the re-key
below), menu (46 items), vendors (9), monthly budget, calendar, dayparts,
dish-name alias map, location-code bridge, plus 500-row samples of each fact.
The facts themselves are regenerated at setup into `data/generated/` and pinned
by `seeds/manifest.sha256`.

## Determinism contract

`python -m generator` is byte-reproducible: fixed seed, one numpy PCG64 stream
consumed in a fixed pipeline order, money as integer cents end to end,
sign-aware rendering, LF line endings emitted by the writer itself, no
directory iteration, no wall clock, hardcoded historical DST offsets instead of
a tzdata dependency. The exact numpy pin in `requirements.txt` is load-bearing:
NumPy guarantees bit-generator stream stability but may change Generator
distribution methods between feature releases. The workflow in
`.github/workflows/dataset-determinism.yml` regenerates the dataset on Linux
and fails if committed seeds change (`git status --porcelain`) or fact
checksums drift (`python -m generator --verify`).

## The injected mess catalog

Each item is a named, parameterized block in `generator/config.py` and maps to
a documented cleaning step (the staging checklists in `sql/staging/`):

1. **Dish-name drift**: a share of lines for 8 dishes use realistic POS
   variants (truncations, misspellings, casings); the converted store's
   re-keyed POS introduces fresh variants. `seeds/ref_item_aliases.csv` is
   built from the same variant table the generator draws from, so a total
   inner join with a zero-row anti-join audit is achievable.
2. **POS-vs-GL gaps**: four windows (deposit shifts, a missing day, penny
   rounding). Outside them, GL ties to cleaned POS net sales exactly.
3. **Re-fired duplicate tickets**: duplicates share the full business key
   (location, business date, ticket, line) and differ only in `closed_at`;
   dedup-keep-latest is the contract.
4. **Late postings**: month-end tickets close 1-2 days late; quarter-end
   invoices post 2-5 weeks after invoice date.
5. **Voids, comps, refunds**: business reality, not defects; the net-sales
   rule (sales net of discounts, refunds negative, voids and comps excluded)
   is applied once, in the mart.
6. **NULL costs**: three rotating menu items carry no cost card; a produce
   vendor window has blank invoice unit costs with extended cost present.
7. **Inconsistent date formats**: two stores exported M/D/YYYY and YYYY/MM/DD
   business dates for stretches of 2024; one store's `closed_at` is UTC
   ISO-8601 for spring 2025 (DST boundary inside the window, on purpose).
8. **Categorical typos**: menu category casings, invoice UoM variants
   (CS/case/Case), labor role casings.
9. **Outliers**: six explicitly planted mis-keys (a $9,999 unit price, qty 99,
   a $0.01 price, a discount larger than its line, a negative sale qty).
   Finance posted corrected amounts, so these also create tie-out variance.
10. **Mid-2025 format conversion + POS re-key**: one store converts
    full-service to counter-service on 2025-07-01; the reinstalled POS issues a
    new location code (`CW-GC` to `CW-GCX`), so raw facts carry two codes for
    one economic entity. Finance systems (GL, payroll, AP, budget) never
    re-keyed. `seeds/ref_location_bridge.csv` resolves 10 codes to 9 locations.
    Naive `GROUP BY location_code` shows a store crashing to zero and a phantom
    store appearing; that is the lesson.

## Planted analytical findings

The generator plants findings as parameter adjustments before drawing (a labor
template that was never rescaled at one store, two vendor beef-price steps, a
held price round on two beef flagships, an opening ramp, an attach-rate gap),
never as pasted output rows. `python -m generator.calibrate` recomputes the
four headline metrics from the generated files, mess and all, and fails if any
leaves its tolerance band; CI runs it on every push. Headline numbers quoted in
the README and the decision memo are transcribed from measured query output,
never from generator parameters.

Two honesty notes carried into the analysis: COGS here is purchases (AP
invoices), a proxy that is only meaningful over 4-plus-week windows because
delivery days are lumpy; streak analysis therefore uses rolling 4-week prime
cost, and the decision memo carries the same caveat. Labor rates are blended,
fully loaded employer costs per role, not offer wages.

## Realism anchors

Calibration targets came from public references: full-service prime-cost
benchmarks near 60-65% of sales, the 2026 NYC wage floor, the well-documented
2025 beef price surge, and typical multi-unit AUV ranges. Anchor list and
links live in the README's methodology section as they enter employer-facing
claims; nothing in this dataset copies any real company's figures.
